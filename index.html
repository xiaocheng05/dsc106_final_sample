US Temperature Changes

A deeper look into temperature trends across the United States

Po-Hua Chen, Xiao Cheng, Haowen Zhang, Claire Tu · CMIP6 Data Source

How Has Temperature Changed?

temperature anomaly is the difference between a year's average temperature and the long-term pre-industrial baseline (1850–1900). A positive anomaly means the year was warmer than that baseline; a negative anomaly means it was cooler. Scroll to explore how the trend has unfolded over 165 years.

The Pre-Industrial Baseline

From 1850 to 1900, global temperatures were relatively stable. This period is our "zero line" — the reference from which all warming is measured, as defined by CMIP6 models and the Paris Agreement.

Early Industrial Warming

As fossil fuel use spread through the early 20th century, temperatures began to creep upward. The warming was gradual at first — partly offset by industrial aerosols that scatter incoming sunlight.

Mid-Century Turbulence

Between 1950 and 1980, heavy industrial pollution produced aerosols that temporarily cooled parts of the planet. Some years even registered negative anomalies — a brief pause before the dominant trend reasserted itself.

The Unmistakable Trend

From the 1980s onward, the warming is unambiguous. Clean air regulations reduced the aerosol shield, letting the greenhouse effect take hold. Nearly every recent year sits above the pre-industrial baseline.

What are we comparing?

Climate change is shaped by more than one factor. In this project, we compare surface temperature, aerosol concentration, incoming solar radiation, and carbon dioxide to explore how different atmospheric variables relate to temperature change.

tas

Surface Air Temperature

This measures air temperature near Earth's surface. It is our main outcome variable: when tas rises, it means the surface climate is getting warmer.

This is the temperature signal we are trying to explain.

od550aer

Aerosol Optical Depth

This measures how much tiny airborne particles block or scatter light. Higher values suggest more aerosols in the atmosphere.

Impact on temperature: aerosols can reduce incoming sunlight and create short-term cooling or climate disruption.

rsdt

Incoming Solar Radiation

This measures solar energy reaching the top of the atmosphere. It represents the amount of incoming energy entering the climate system.

Impact on temperature: more incoming solar energy can support warming, but its effect can be complicated by clouds, aerosols, and greenhouse gases.

co2

Carbon Dioxide

CO₂ traps heat in the atmosphere. Unlike aerosols, its warming effect is more persistent and builds over time.

Impact on temperature: CO₂ is an important driver of long-term warming.

The key question is not whether one variable explains everything. Instead, we ask how these factors appear to contribute to temperature change, and where the long-term warming signal remains visible.

      Key Takeaways

      🗺️

      States do not warm for the same reasons. Our regression model shows that CO₂, aerosols, and solar radiation together explain 76% of Wyoming's warming — but only 18% of Mississippi's. The drivers that matter most are not the same everywhere.

      🌲
      Western states like Oregon and Montana are most sensitive to CO₂ and also show the strongest aerosol cooling effects — aerosols there actively suppress warming.
      🏜️
      Arizona is an outlier: aerosols are associated with warming there, not cooling — the opposite of most states.
      📉
      Gulf Coast states (Mississippi, Louisiana) follow patterns our three-variable model barely explains, suggesting local factors dominate.

      One Trend, Many Stories

      The global warming signal is real and visible in every state — but the mix of causes shifts dramatically by region. A policy that targets only CO₂ may miss the picture in states where aerosols or local dynamics are the decisive factor.
