How Has Temperature Changed?

Temperature anomaly measures how much warmer or cooler a year is compared to a baseline average. This chart uses the 1850–1900 pre-industrial period as the baseline, the same reference adopted by CMIP6 climate models and the Paris Agreement. A positive anomaly means that year was warmer than pre-industrial levels; a negative anomaly means it was cooler. Red bars show warmer years; blue bars show cooler ones.

What are we comparing?

Climate change is shaped by more than one factor. In this project, we compare surface temperature, aerosol concentration, incoming solar radiation, and carbon dioxide to explore how different atmospheric variables relate to temperature change.

tas

Surface Air Temperature

This measures air temperature near Earth's surface. It is our main outcome variable: when tas rises, it means the surface climate is getting warmer.

This is the temperature signal we are trying to explain.

od550aer

Aerosol Optical Depth

This measures how much tiny airborne particles block or scatter light. Higher values suggest more aerosols in the atmosphere.

Impact on temperature: aerosols can reduce incoming sunlight and create short-term cooling or climate disruption.

rsdt

Incoming Solar Radiation

This measures solar energy reaching the top of the atmosphere. It represents the amount of incoming energy entering the climate system.

Impact on temperature: more incoming solar energy can support warming, but its effect can be complicated by clouds, aerosols, and greenhouse gases.

co2

Carbon Dioxide

CO₂ traps heat in the atmosphere. Unlike aerosols, its warming effect is more persistent and builds over time.

Impact on temperature: CO₂ is an important driver of long-term warming.

The key question is not whether one variable explains everything. Instead, we ask how these factors appear to contribute to temperature change, and where the long-term warming signal remains visible.
